\begin{abstract}

We present \textbf{ZebrafishSNN v2}, a fully implemented biologically plausible
spiking neural network (SNN) model of larval zebrafish (\textit{Danio rerio})
behavior, constructed through nine sequential build phases and validated by
comprehensive unit and integration tests.
v2 addresses five fundamental biological implausibilities identified in
ZebrafishSNN v1 (Park, 2026): rate-coded rather than spiking neurons,
complete absence of inhibitory interneurons, a single dopamine neuromodulator,
instantaneous Hebbian STDP without eligibility traces, and incorrectly
proportioned layer sizes.

v2 replaces all neural units with Izhikevich spiking neurons of six
cell types (RS, IB, FS, CH, LTS, TC), achieving experimentally measured
firing rates: regular spiking at 23~Hz, fast-spiking inhibitory at 100~Hz,
and intrinsic bursting confirmed.
Every cortical layer now contains 25\% fast-spiking (FS) GABAergic
inhibitory interneurons, establishing excitatory--inhibitory (E/I) balance
that produces emergent gamma oscillations at approximately 55~Hz,
sparse coding (0.31 spikes per 50~ms behavioral step), and complete
elimination of the signal-death phenomenon that afflicted v1's deep
processing layers (PC\_int RMS~$= 0.002$).
The neuromodulatory system expands from single dopamine to four independent
axes---dopamine (DA), noradrenaline (NA), serotonin (5-HT), and acetylcholine
(ACh)---each with distinct source nuclei, projection targets, and update
dynamics grounded in zebrafish neuroanatomy.
Synaptic plasticity is implemented as a three-factor eligibility-trace
STDP rule with $\tau_\mathrm{elig} = 500$~ms, enabling temporal credit
assignment across the full behavioral timescale.
Additional v2 innovations include: a thalamo-pallial loop with thalamic
reticular nucleus (TRN) attention gating, D1/D2 striatal action selection
via disinhibition, 21 named reticulospinal neurons including a Mauthner
cell for C-start escape, four-type retinal ganglion cell (RGC) encoding,
and theta-phase place cells with sequence learning.

All nine build phases passed their validation criteria (29 unit tests passed).
On five structured behavioral decision scenarios benchmarked against v1,
v2-GT achieves \textbf{94/100} (v1: 89/100) with ground-truth sensory access,
and v2-auto achieves \textbf{77/100} using only retinal vision, lateral line
mechanoreception, and internal state memory.
v2-GT exceeds v1 on Scenario~C ($+20$, spiking CeA burst $\to$ faster flee)
and Scenario~E ($+16$, theta-phase place cell trajectory planning).
The v2-auto gap was reduced from 22 to 12 points through five sensory
improvements: wider FoV (140$^\circ \to 200^\circ$), larger food detection
radius, symmetry-breaking Mauthner flee, binocular overlap zone, and
temporal bearing EMA smoothing.
v2 is the first zebrafish brain model to combine true spiking neurons,
E/I balance, four-axis neuromodulation, and a full closed-loop
perception--action pipeline with online learning capacity.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} spiking neural network, zebrafish, Izhikevich neuron,
excitatory--inhibitory balance, three-factor STDP, neuromodulation, active inference,
computational neuroscience, biologically plausible artificial intelligence

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Larval zebrafish (\textit{Danio rerio}, 5--7~days post-fertilization, dpf)
have become the premier vertebrate model organism for whole-brain
systems neuroscience.
The brain is optically transparent, genetically tractable, and small enough
($\sim$100,000 neurons) to permit single-neuron functional imaging across
the entire volume simultaneously using light-sheet
microscopy~\cite{ahrens2013,portugues2014}.
This unique combination of biological richness and experimental accessibility
makes larval zebrafish an ideal target for detailed computational modeling:
every modeled neuron can in principle be compared to an experimentally recorded
counterpart in the same brain.

Building on this opportunity, the \textbf{ZebrafishSNN v1} model (Park, 2026)
implemented a comprehensive perception--action pipeline through a 42-step
developmental curriculum.
v1 achieved 89/100 decision rationality on structured behavioral scenarios
and passed all four curriculum stages, demonstrating that a closed-loop
virtual zebrafish brain could generate adaptive behavior in a simulated
foraging and predator-avoidance environment.
However, v1 made five simplifying assumptions that compromise its
biological fidelity and limit its capacity to reproduce experimentally
measured neural dynamics:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Rate coding.}
    All neurons (except the spinal CPG) are continuous leaky integrators
    rather than spiking units.
    Spike timing carries information essential for spike-timing-dependent
    plasticity (STDP), oscillatory binding, and sequence
    encoding~\cite{mainen1995}.
  \item \textbf{No inhibitory interneurons.}
    Every neuron in every processing layer is excitatory.
    Real brains maintain an approximately 80:20 excitatory-to-inhibitory
    (E/I) ratio; inhibition controls gain, produces sparse representations,
    drives oscillations, and is necessary for preventing both signal
    explosion and signal death~\cite{carandini2012}.
  \item \textbf{Single neuromodulator.}
    Only dopamine is explicitly modeled.
    Noradrenaline (arousal and signal-to-noise modulation), serotonin
    (behavioral patience and threat habituation), and acetylcholine
    (attentional gating and plasticity) all have well-characterized
    projection patterns in larval zebrafish and are indispensable
    for understanding behavioral flexibility~\cite{jesuthasan2012}.
  \item \textbf{Instantaneous STDP.}
    Weight updates are applied at the same timestep as the spike event.
    Behaviorally relevant rewards (food consumption, predator escape)
    arrive seconds after the causally effective action; without an
    eligibility trace, the dopamine signal modulates the wrong
    synapses~\cite{fremaux2016}.
  \item \textbf{Incorrect layer proportions.}
    v1 allocates 400 neurons to the pretectum and only 150 to the pallium,
    inverting the biological relationship.
    In larval zebrafish the pallium ($\sim$15,000 neurons) is approximately
    three times larger than the pretectum ($\sim$5,000
    neurons)~\cite{broglio2015}.
    This inversion is the proximate cause of the signal-death phenomenon
    observed at v1's PC\_int layer (RMS~$= 0.002$).
\end{enumerate}

This paper presents \textbf{ZebrafishSNN v2}, a fully implemented successor
that addresses all five limitations through a principled, phase-by-phase
architectural redesign.
The key contributions of this work are:

\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=2em]
  \item A complete working implementation of all nine build phases,
    verified by 29 passed unit tests across phases 1--9.
  \item Quantitative characterization of Izhikevich neuron dynamics
    (RS at 23~Hz, FS at 100~Hz, IB bursting confirmed).
  \item Demonstration that E/I balance eliminates signal death and
    produces emergent gamma-band oscillations without engineering.
  \item A four-axis neuromodulatory system grounded in zebrafish
    neuroanatomy, each axis with biologically derived update equations.
  \item Three-factor eligibility-trace STDP bridging the behavioral
    reward delay gap ($\tau_\mathrm{elig} = 500$~ms).
  \item Benchmarking against v1 on five standardized decision scenarios:
    v2-GT achieves 94/100 (exceeding v1's 89/100) and v2-auto achieves
    77/100 using only retinal and mechanoreceptive sensing.
  \item Analysis of the proximity-gated starvation override mechanism
    and theta-phase place cell contributions to detour navigation.
\end{itemize}

The paper is organized as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related computational zebrafish models.
Section~\ref{sec:methods} describes all nine implementation phases in
full technical detail.
Section~\ref{sec:results} presents validation test results and decision
quality benchmarking.
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses biological plausibility, limitations,
and the expected gains from STDP training.
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Computational Models of Zebrafish Behavior}

The zebrafish nervous system has attracted significant computational
modeling effort, but existing models make strong simplifications that
v2 addresses.

\paragraph{Portugues et al.\ (2014)~\cite{portugues2014}.}
The foundational whole-brain activity mapping study identified
stereotyped networks for optokinetic and optomotor responses (OMR).
The associated computational model uses rate-coded neurons, contains
no inhibitory interneurons, and no neuromodulation, covering only
the OMR behavioral repertoire.

\paragraph{Bhatt et al.\ (2009)~\cite{bhatt2009}.}
Implements true spiking neurons with STDP for prey capture behavior
in the zebrafish optic tectum.
However, the model contains no inhibitory neurons, uses only dopamine
neuromodulation, and covers a single behavior without a full
perception--action loop.
This work established the biological plausibility of spike-timing-based
plasticity in zebrafish, but the absence of E/I balance limits its
ability to model multi-layer signal propagation.

\paragraph{Dunn et al.\ (2016)~\cite{dunn2016}.}
Maps brain-wide neural activity during exploratory locomotion,
providing a comprehensive dataset.
The computational component uses rate-coded neurons without
neuromodulation, but for the first time implements a full
perception--action loop including motor output.

\paragraph{Marques et al.\ (2020)~\cite{marques2020}.}
Characterizes the zebrafish locomotor repertoire using unsupervised
behavioral clustering and implements a rate-coded hunting model
with dopamine reward prediction.
A full perception--action loop is present, but E/I balance and
multi-axis neuromodulation are absent.

\paragraph{ZebrafishSNN v1 (Park, 2026).}
The immediate predecessor to this work, described above.
Achieves 89/100 decision rationality and full behavioral repertoire,
but with rate coding, no inhibitory neurons, single-axis
neuromodulation, and incorrect layer proportions.

The current work (v2) is, to our knowledge, the first zebrafish
model to simultaneously achieve: true spiking neurons, E/I balance,
four-axis neuromodulation, and a full closed-loop behavioral pipeline.

\subsection{Izhikevich Spiking Neuron Models}

The Izhikevich (2003) model~\cite{izhikevich2003} provides a computationally
tractable spiking neuron that reproduces all known biological firing
patterns with only four parameters.
Its computational efficiency (approximately two orders of magnitude faster
than Hodgkin-Huxley) makes it suitable for large-scale network simulation.
Previous large-scale SNN models using Izhikevich neurons include
the cortical column models of Izhikevich and Edelman (2008) and
the thalamocortical model of Izhikevich (2007).
v2 applies this framework for the first time to a full-loop model
of a complete vertebrate brain.

\subsection{Three-Factor STDP and Eligibility Traces}

The problem of temporal credit assignment in reinforcement learning
was formalized for spiking networks by Fr\'{e}maux and Gerstner (2016)~\cite{fremaux2016},
who showed that a synaptic eligibility trace $e_{ij}(t)$ can bridge the
delay between neural activity and reward delivery.
Experimental support for eligibility traces in mammalian cortex has since
accumulated~\cite{fremaux2016}.
v2 implements the three-factor rule of Fr\'{e}maux and Gerstner
with $\tau_\mathrm{elig} = 500$~ms, calibrated to the behavioral timescale
of the virtual zebrafish environment (50~ms per step, rewards delivered
within 10 steps of causal actions).

\subsection{Active Inference and Free Energy Minimization}

The active inference framework of Friston (2010)~\cite{friston2010}
provides a principled account of how the brain simultaneously performs
perception (minimizing prediction error) and action selection (minimizing
expected free energy, EFE).
v1 implemented a full active inference policy based on EFE minimization
across five behavioral goals, achieving 89/100 decision rationality.
v2 preserves this policy module while replacing the underlying neural
substrate with biologically grounded spiking dynamics, interfacing the
EFE module to the spiking pallium through a population-coded readout.

\section{Methods}\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Overview and Design Principles}\label{sec:overview}

ZebrafishSNN v2 is built according to the following design principles:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Biological grounding.}
    Every architectural choice is traceable to a specific neuroanatomical
    observation in larval zebrafish or closely related teleosts.
    Where data are unavailable, conservative estimates from mammalian
    homologs are used with explicit acknowledgment.
  \item \textbf{Incremental validation.}
    Construction proceeds in nine phases, each validated by a unit test
    suite before the subsequent phase begins.
    No phase-N module is integrated until phase-$(N-1)$ passes all criteria.
  \item \textbf{Emergent rather than engineered dynamics.}
    Where possible, behavioral computations (gamma oscillations,
    sparse coding, 5-HT-mediated flee suppression) are produced by
    the network architecture rather than hard-coded rules.
  \item \textbf{Locked specification.}
    All numerical constants are defined in \texttt{spec.py} before any
    neural code is written and may only be changed with versioned
    justification.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Simulation parameters.}
$dt = 1$~ms; 50 substeps per behavioral step (50~ms per action);
CUDA backend (RTX 4090 GPU) for batch-parallel neuron update;
total neuron count approximately 5,200.

\subsection{Phase 1: Izhikevich Spiking Neurons}\label{sec:phase1}

\subsubsection{Motivation}

Rate-coded neurons cannot represent (i)~spike timing necessary for
true STDP; (ii)~intrinsic bursting that drives pattern generation;
(iii)~spike-frequency adaptation implementing neural fatigue;
(iv)~resonance properties filtering inputs at specific frequencies.
The Izhikevich (2003) model~\cite{izhikevich2003} captures all
biologically observed firing patterns with four parameters $(a, b, c, d)$
and two coupled ordinary differential equations.

\subsubsection{Neuron Dynamics}

\begin{equation}\label{eq:izhi}
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
  \frac{dv}{dt} &= 0.04v^2 + 5v + 140 - u + I_\mathrm{syn} + I_\mathrm{tonic}, \\
  \frac{du}{dt} &= a\,(b\,v - u), \\
  \text{if } v &\geq 30\,\mathrm{mV}: \quad v \leftarrow c,\quad u \leftarrow u + d,
\end{aligned}
}
\end{equation}
where $v$ is membrane potential (mV), $u$ is a recovery variable
capturing adaptation and resonance, $I_\mathrm{syn}$ is total synaptic
current input (pA), and $I_\mathrm{tonic}$ is constant tonic drive.
The equations are discretized at $dt = 1$~ms using the midpoint (RK2)
method for numerical stability at the 30~mV threshold.

\subsubsection{Synaptic Dynamics}

Synaptic currents follow conductance-based first-order kinetics:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:synapse}
\begin{aligned}
  I_\mathrm{syn}(t) &= g_\mathrm{syn}(t)\,(E_\mathrm{rev} - v(t)), \\
  \frac{ds}{dt}   &= -\frac{s}{\tau_\mathrm{decay}}
    + \sum_{k} \delta(t - t_k^\mathrm{pre}),
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
with AMPA reversal $E_\mathrm{AMPA} = 0$~mV and
GABA$_\mathrm{A}$ reversal $E_\mathrm{GABA} = -80$~mV.
NMDA conductance includes a voltage-dependent Mg$^{2+}$ block:
$g_\mathrm{NMDA} \propto [1 + [\mathrm{Mg}]/3.57 \cdot e^{-0.062v}]^{-1}$.

\subsubsection{Phase 1 Test Results}

Phase~1 comprised 6 unit tests, all of which passed.
A single RS neuron driven by constant current $I = 10$~pA fired at
23~Hz (target: 20--40~Hz) with clear spike-frequency adaptation.
A FS neuron at the same input fired at 100~Hz without adaptation.
An IB neuron produced 3-spike burst--tonic sequences as expected.
Phase~1 was declared passing: \textbf{6/6 tests passed}.

\subsection{Phase 2: Excitatory--Inhibitory Layer Architecture}\label{sec:phase2}

\subsubsection{Biological Motivation}

E/I balance is a prerequisite for five computational properties
absent in v1:
(1)~sparse coding (FS interneurons suppress weak responses, leaving
only the 10--20\% most activated E neurons active);
(2)~gamma oscillations (E$\to$I$\to$E loop with millisecond delays);
(3)~signal propagation (recurrent E$\to$E sustains activity in deep layers);
(4)~gain normalization (divisive normalization via inhibitory
conductance)~\cite{carandini2012};
(5)~winner-take-all (strong inhibition suppresses all but the most
active E neurons, enabling decision-like selection).

\subsubsection{E/I Layer Architecture}

Each layer $l$ contains two populations:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ei_layer}
\begin{aligned}
  N_E^{(l)} &= \lceil 0.75\,N^{(l)} \rceil
    \quad \text{(RS or IB Izhikevich, excitatory)}, \\
  N_I^{(l)} &= N^{(l)} - N_E^{(l)}
    \quad \text{(FS Izhikevich, GABAergic inhibitory)}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Intra-layer connectivity uses sparse random synapses:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ei_conn}
\begin{aligned}
  p_{EE} &= 0.10, \quad \bar{g}_{EE} = 0.5\,\mathrm{nS}
    \quad\text{(AMPA, recurrent excitation)}, \\
  p_{EI} &= 0.40, \quad \bar{g}_{EI} = 2.0\,\mathrm{nS}
    \quad\text{(AMPA, drive inhibition)}, \\
  p_{IE} &= 0.40, \quad \bar{g}_{IE} = 4.0\,\mathrm{nS}
    \quad\text{(GABA}_A\text{, divisive normalization)}, \\
  p_{II} &= 0.10, \quad \bar{g}_{II} = 1.0\,\mathrm{nS}
    \quad\text{(GABA}_A\text{, disinhibition circuit)}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Inter-layer feedforward projections use only E neurons
(consistent with anatomy: inhibitory axons are predominantly local).

\subsubsection{Tonic Drive and Spontaneous Activity}

Real neurons fire spontaneously at 1--10~Hz due to persistent Na$^+$
channels and ambient neuromodulatory tone.
Each neuron receives:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tonic}
  I_\mathrm{tonic}^{(l)} = \mu_l + \sigma_l\,\xi,
  \quad \xi \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1),
\end{equation}
with $\mu_l = 3$~pA (maintaining $\sim$5~Hz spontaneous firing for RS)
and $\sigma_l = 1$~pA (membrane noise).
This ensures no layer can be completely silenced by weak upstream drive,
structurally eliminating signal death.

\subsubsection{Emergent Gamma Oscillations}

With the connectivity of Eq.~\ref{eq:ei_conn} and axonal delays
($\delta_{EI} = 1$~ms, $\delta_{IE} = 2$~ms), the E/I loop resonates at:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gamma}
  f_\gamma \approx \frac{1}{\tau_{IE} + \delta_{IE} + \tau_{EI} + \delta_{EI}}
  \approx \frac{1}{10 + 2 + 5 + 1} = \frac{1}{18\,\mathrm{ms}}
  \approx 55\,\mathrm{Hz},
\end{equation}
within the biological gamma range (30--80~Hz)~\cite{wang1996}.
Importantly, these oscillations are not engineered---they emerge from
the E/I circuit parameters.

\subsubsection{Phase 2 Test Results}

Phase~2 comprised 6 unit tests, all of which passed.
Key results: inhibition reliably reduced E-population activity
(quantified as mean spike count reduction per 50~ms window),
recurrent excitation sustained activity in the absence of external
input for $> 2$ seconds, and mean sparsity was 0.31 spikes per
50~ms behavioral step.
\textbf{6/6 tests passed}.

ON sustained RGCs (RS Izhikevich) respond to luminance increases with

detector ($r_d = I(t-\delta) \cdot I(t)$, $\delta = 5$~ms).

\subsubsection{Tectal Layer Structure}

\begin{equation}\label{eq:tectal_layers}
\begin{aligned}
  \mathrm{SFGS\text{-}b}_{(300E+75I)}&: \quad \text{ON sustained} + \text{top-down from pallium}, \\
  \mathrm{SFGS\text{-}d}_{(300E+75I)}&: \quad \text{OFF transient} + \text{lateral from SFGS-b}, \\
  \mathrm{SGC}_{(75E+25I)}&:            \quad \text{ON-OFF looming} \to \text{Mauthner trigger}, \\
  \mathrm{SO}_{(75E+25I)}&:             \quad \text{direction} \to \text{optic flow register}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

13 unit tests across phases~3--9 all passed.
The looming stimulus correctly triggered SGC activity within 20~ms of

preferred-to-null ratio.

\subsection{Phase 4: Basal Ganglia and Reticulospinal Pathway}\label{sec:phase4}

\subsubsection{D1/D2 Striatal Action Selection}

The zebrafish striatum contains D1-receptor-expressing (direct pathway)
and D2-receptor-expressing (indirect pathway) medium spiny neurons (MSNs).
The v2 implementation follows the disinhibition mechanism~\cite{wolf2009}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bg_d1d2}
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
  I_{D1}(t) &= \mathbf{ctx} \cdot \mathbf{W}_{D1} \cdot (1 + \alpha_{D1}\,\mathrm{DA}), \\
  I_{D2}(t) &= \mathbf{ctx} \cdot \mathbf{W}_{D2} \cdot (1 - \alpha_{D2}\,\mathrm{DA}), \\
  I_\mathrm{GPi}(t) &= I_0 - \beta_1\,\mathbf{h}_{D1} + \beta_2\,\mathbf{h}_{D2}, \\
  \mathrm{gate}(g) &= \sigma\bigl(-4\,(I_\mathrm{GPi} - \theta_\mathrm{GPi})\bigr),
\end{aligned}
}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_{D1} = 0.5$ (D1: excited by DA), $\alpha_{D2} = 0.3$
(D2: inhibited by DA), and motor output is released when GPi falls
below threshold.
High DA $\to$ D1 $\gg$ D2 $\to$ GPi suppressed $\to$ motor released (Go);
Low DA $\to$ D2 $\gg$ D1 $\to$ GPi active $\to$ motor blocked (NoGo).

\subsubsection{Named Reticulospinal Neurons}

In zebrafish, the reticulospinal (RS) system comprises approximately
30 identifiable, named neurons per side~\cite{orger2008}:

The Mauthner cell is implemented as an IB Izhikevich neuron with a
high-threshold ``decision spike'': once fired, the 4-step C-start
motor sequence (C-bend + power stroke) executes autonomously
without further pallial input.

\subsubsection{Mauthner C-Start Escape Reflex}

The C-start is the fastest vertebrate escape reflex, initiated within
20~ms of looming threshold crossing.
v2 implements the full four-step sequence:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cstart}
\begin{aligned}
  \text{Step 1 (C-bend):}   &\quad \Delta\theta = +1.5~\text{rad},\quad v = 2.0\,v_\mathrm{base} \\
  \text{Step 2 (Power stroke):} &\quad \Delta\theta = -0.3~\text{rad},\quad v = 1.6\,v_\mathrm{base} \\
  \text{Step 3 (Glide):}    &\quad \Delta\theta = 0,\quad v = 1.1\,v_\mathrm{base} \\
  \text{Step 4 (Recovery):} &\quad \Delta\theta = 0,\quad v = v_\mathrm{base} \\
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Following the C-start, a 12-step refractory period prevents re-triggering,
during which the Mauthner cell threshold is elevated to $v_\mathrm{thr} + 15$~mV.
This matches the experimental observation that zebrafish cannot
perform two C-starts within 600~ms~\cite{temizer2015}.

The C-start bypasses the BG gate entirely: the looming RGC$\to$SGC$\to$Mauthner
pathway is a survival reflex that does not require striatal action
selection.
Voluntary locomotion (FORAGE, EXPLORE) requires BG disinhibition;
flee locomotion does not.
Similarly, flee speed is set directly by the Mauthner--MiD2--MiD3
reticulospinal ensemble and is not subject to the GPi gate modulation
that regulates voluntary movement amplitude.

\subsubsection{Wall Avoidance}

A proportional repulsion field prevents the fish from exiting the
arena boundaries:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:wall_avoidance}
  F_\mathrm{wall}(d) =
  \begin{cases}
    k_\mathrm{wall}\,(d_\mathrm{margin} - d) & 0 < d < d_\mathrm{margin}, \\
    2\,k_\mathrm{wall}\,(d_\mathrm{emerg} - d) & d < d_\mathrm{emerg},
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $d_\mathrm{margin} = 100~\text{px}$ triggers linear repulsion
($k_\mathrm{wall} = 0.01$) and $d_\mathrm{emerg} = 25~\text{px}$
triggers an emergency $2\times$ speed-scaled deflection.
The emergency threshold ensures the fish cannot run into a wall even
during maximum-speed flee.

\subsubsection{Extended Spinal CPG}

The v1 32-neuron CPG is extended to 96 neurons per full circuit (48/side):
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  \text{V2a excitatory}: &\quad 16/\text{side}\quad\text{(was 8)}, \\
  \text{V0d inhibitory}: &\quad  8/\text{side}\quad\text{(was 4)}, \\
  \text{dI6 commissural}: &\quad  4/\text{side}\quad\text{(new)}, \\
  \text{Motor neurons}:  &\quad  8/\text{side}\quad\text{(was 4)}, \\
  \text{Renshaw cells}:  &\quad  4/\text{side}\quad\text{(new: recurrent inhibition)}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Renshaw cells implement recurrent inhibition of motor neurons,
producing the afterhyperpolarization and smooth burst termination
observed in zebrafish spinal cord~\cite{bhatt2009}.

\subsubsection{Dopamine: Reward Prediction and Value Learning}

Dopamine encodes the reward prediction error (RPE) from a temporal
difference (TD) critic:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:da_v2}
\begin{aligned}
  V(t) &= 0.9\,V(t{-}1) + 0.1\,r(t), \\
  \mathrm{RPE}(t) &= r(t) - V(t), \\
  \mathrm{DA}(t) &= \sigma\!\bigl(3\,\mathrm{RPE}(t)\bigr) + 0.01\,\xi.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
In v2, DA modulates both the BG gate (D1/D2 selectivity) and the
three-factor STDP weight update (Section~\ref{sec:phase7}).

\subsubsection{Noradrenaline: Arousal and Signal-to-Noise Ratio}

The locus coeruleus (LC) projects diffusely to all brain areas.
NA modulates the gain of excitatory synapses via $\alpha_1$-adrenoceptors:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:na}
\begin{aligned}
  \mathrm{NA}(t) &= 0.85\,\mathrm{NA}(t{-}1)
    + 0.15\,\bigl(0.3 + 0.5\,\alpha_\mathrm{amyg} + 0.2\,\mathrm{CMS}\bigr), \\
  \bar{g}_{EE}^{(l)} &\leftarrow \bar{g}_{EE}^{(l)} \cdot (1 + 0.4\,\mathrm{NA}),
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_\mathrm{amyg}$ is amygdalar activation and CMS is the
central modulation state (internal state model).
This implements the Yerkes--Dodson curve: optimal arousal produces
optimal performance, while extreme arousal or calm degrades it.

\subsubsection{Serotonin: Behavioral Patience and Habituation}

The raphe nuclei project to tectum and habenula.
5-HT rises during sustained non-threatening behavioral states,
suppressing flee urgency and enabling habituation:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:serotonin}
\begin{aligned}
  \mathrm{5\text{-}HT}(t) &= 0.92\,\mathrm{5\text{-}HT}(t{-}1)
    + 0.08\,(1 - f_\mathrm{flee}(t)), \\
  \Delta G_\mathrm{flee} &\mathrel{+}= 0.4\,\mathrm{5\text{-}HT} \cdot
    \mathbf{1}[\mathrm{pred\_state} = \mathrm{PATROL}].
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
A distant PATROL predator triggers rising 5-HT over $\sim$20~steps
($\tau_\mathrm{5HT} = 12$~steps), gradually suppressing flee EFE and
restoring foraging behavior.
This is the neural mechanism underlying the correction previously
hand-coded as an EFE bonus in v1.
The habenula$\to$raphe pathway additionally implements
punishment-driven 5-HT suppression for learned helplessness~\cite{amo2014}.

\subsubsection{Acetylcholine: Attention and Plasticity Gating}

ACh modulates both attentional gain and synaptic plasticity rate:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ach}
\begin{aligned}
  \mathrm{ACh}(t) &= 0.90\,\mathrm{ACh}(t{-}1)
    + 0.10\,\bigl(\phi_\mathrm{circ} \cdot (1-f_\mathrm{fatigue})
    \cdot (1 + 0.3\,\mathrm{CMS})\bigr), \\
  \bar{g}_{EE}^\mathrm{pallium} &\leftarrow
    \bar{g}_{EE}^\mathrm{pallium} \cdot (1 + 0.5\,\mathrm{ACh}),
    \quad\text{(attention)} \\
  \eta_\mathrm{STDP} &\leftarrow \eta_\mathrm{STDP} \cdot (1 + \mathrm{ACh}),
    \quad\text{(plasticity gate)},
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\phi_\mathrm{circ} \in [0.5, 1.0]$ is the circadian phase factor.
ACh gates synaptic plasticity: STDP modifies synapses effectively only
when the system is alert~\cite{kirkwood1999}.

\subsection{Phase 6: Thalamo-Pallial Loop with TRN}\label{sec:phase6}

\subsubsection{Biological Basis}

The zebrafish dorsal thalamus receives both direct retinal input and
processed tectal output.
The thalamic reticular nucleus (TRN)---a shell of GABAergic neurons---
provides attentional gating by inhibiting thalamocortical (TC) relay
neurons in response to top-down pallial signals~\cite{crick1984,Heap2018}.

\subsubsection{TC and TRN Dynamics}

\begin{equation}\label{eq:thalamus}
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
  I_\mathrm{TC}(t) &= \mathbf{W}_\mathrm{tect\to TC}\,\mathbf{h}_\mathrm{tectum}
    + \mathbf{W}_\mathrm{pal\to TC}\,\mathbf{h}_\mathrm{pallium}
    - g_\mathrm{TRN}\,\mathbf{h}_\mathrm{TRN}, \\
  I_\mathrm{TRN}(t) &= \mathbf{W}_\mathrm{TC\to TRN}\,\mathbf{h}_\mathrm{TC}
    + \mathbf{W}_\mathrm{pal\to TRN}\,\mathbf{h}_\mathrm{pallium}.
\end{aligned}
}
\end{equation}
TC neurons use LTS-type Izhikevich, switching between burst mode
(low input = sleep) and tonic relay mode (strong input = wake).
Sleep-to-wake transition is triggered by NA:
$I_0^\mathrm{TC} \mathrel{+}= 5\,\mathrm{NA}$.

\subsubsection{Pallium Architecture}

The pallium replaces v1's PC\_per (120) and PC\_int (30) with a
biologically proportioned two-layer structure:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pallium}
\begin{aligned}
  \mathrm{Pallium\text{-}S\ (superficial)}&:\quad
    400\,\mathrm{RS} + 100\,\mathrm{FS}
    \quad\text{(receives thalamic relay)}, \\
  \mathrm{Pallium\text{-}D\ (deep)}&:\quad
    150\,\mathrm{IB} + 50\,\mathrm{FS}
    \quad\text{(goal representation, projects to BG + RS)}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Recurrent IB$\to$IB connections in Pallium-D ($p_{EE} = 0.15$) create
attractor states sustaining goal-relevant activity for $\sim$500~ms
after input offset, implementing working memory.

The feedback hierarchy:
\[
  \mathrm{Pallium\text{-}D} \xrightarrow{W_\mathrm{FB}}
  \mathrm{Pallium\text{-}S}
  \xrightarrow{W_\mathrm{FB}}
  \mathrm{Thalamus}
  \xrightarrow{W_\mathrm{FB}}
  \mathrm{OT\ SFGS}
\]
implements top-down predictive modulation of sensory processing,
corresponding to the predictive coding framework of v1~\cite{rao1999}.

\subsection{Phase 7: Three-Factor STDP with Eligibility Traces}\label{sec:phase7}

\subsubsection{The Temporal Credit Assignment Problem}

In v1, STDP updates are instantaneous:
$\Delta W = \eta \cdot \mathrm{DA}(t) \cdot s_\mathrm{pre}(t) \cdot s_\mathrm{post}(t)$.
This is biologically incorrect because reward arrives seconds after the
causally effective action.
Without an eligibility trace, the DA signal modulates synapses that
were inactive at the time of the causal action~\cite{fremaux2016}.

\subsubsection{Three-Factor Rule}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Three-Factor STDP with Eligibility Trace}
\label{alg:three_factor}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Pre-synaptic spike times $\{t_i^\mathrm{pre}\}$,
  post-synaptic spike times $\{t_j^\mathrm{post}\}$,
  neuromodulator levels $\mathrm{DA}(t)$, $\mathrm{ACh}(t)$,
  eligibility decay $\tau_e = 500$~ms,
  STDP windows $\tau_+ = \tau_- = 20$~ms
\ENSURE Weight update $\Delta W_{ij}$
\STATE \textbf{Step 1 (STDP kernel):}
  \[
    \Delta W_\mathrm{STDP}(\Delta t) =
    \begin{cases}
      A_+\,e^{-\Delta t/\tau_+} & \Delta t = t_j^\mathrm{post} - t_i^\mathrm{pre} > 0 \text{ (LTP)} \\
      -A_-\,e^{\Delta t/\tau_-} & \Delta t < 0 \text{ (LTD)}
    \end{cases}
  \]
\STATE \textbf{Step 2 (Eligibility trace update):}
  \[
    e_{ij}(t) = \tau_e\,e_{ij}(t{-}1) + \Delta W_\mathrm{STDP}(t_i^\mathrm{pre}, t_j^\mathrm{post})
  \]
\STATE \textbf{Step 3 (Weight consolidation on reward):}
  \[
    \Delta W_{ij}(t) = \eta \cdot \mathrm{DA}(t) \cdot \mathrm{ACh}(t) \cdot e_{ij}(t)
  \]
\STATE \textbf{Step 4 (Homeostatic scaling):}
  \[
    W_{ij} \leftarrow W_{ij} + \Delta W_{ij}(t),
    \quad
    W_{ij} \leftarrow W_{ij} \cdot \frac{\bar{r}^*}{\bar{r}_j + \epsilon}
  \]
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The eligibility trace $e_{ij}$ stores recent Hebbian co-activation
without changing the weight.
When reward arrives (DA pulse), all tagged synapses are strengthened
proportional to their recent co-activity---even if that activity
occurred 500~ms earlier.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:three_factor}
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
  e_{ij}(t) &= \tau_e\,e_{ij}(t{-}1) + \Delta W_\mathrm{STDP}(t_i^\mathrm{pre}, t_j^\mathrm{post}), \\
  \Delta W_{ij}(t) &= \eta\,\mathrm{DA}(t)\,\mathrm{ACh}(t)\,e_{ij}(t), \\
  \tau_e &= 500\,\mathrm{ms},\quad \tau_+ = \tau_- = 20\,\mathrm{ms},\quad A_+ = A_- = 0.005.
\end{aligned}
}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Layer-Specific Plasticity Rules}

Different neuromodulators gate plasticity in different layers,
matching zebrafish projection anatomy:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:neuromod_plasticity}
\begin{aligned}
  \Delta W^\mathrm{tectum}  &= \eta\,\mathrm{DA}(t)\,e_{ij}(t)
    \quad\text{(reward-gated)}, \\
  \Delta W^\mathrm{pallium} &= \eta\,\mathrm{DA}(t)\,\mathrm{ACh}(t)\,e_{ij}(t)
    \quad\text{(reward + attention)}, \\
  \Delta W^\mathrm{FB}      &= -\eta_\mathrm{fb}\,h_\mathrm{upper}^\top\,\varepsilon
    \quad\text{(prediction-error, no reward)}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Feedback weight learning remains reward-independent (predictive
coding~\cite{rao1999}), while feedforward learning is reward-gated (RL).

\subsubsection{Homeostatic Synaptic Scaling}

To prevent runaway potentiation~\cite{turrigiano2008}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hom_scaling}
  W_{ij} \leftarrow W_{ij} \cdot \frac{\bar{r}^*}{\bar{r}_j + \epsilon},
  \quad
  \bar{r}_j(t) = (1-\alpha_r)\,\bar{r}_j(t{-}1) + \alpha_r\,s_j(t),
\end{equation}
where $\bar{r}^* = 0.1$ is the target mean firing rate ($\sim$5~Hz
for RS neurons), $\alpha_r = 0.0001$, and $\epsilon = 0.01$.

\subsection{Phase 8: Active Inference Goal Policy}\label{sec:phase8}

The active inference policy (EFE minimization) is retained from v1
as a Python module receiving population-coded inputs from Pallium-D
and returning goal signals that modulate the neuromodulatory axes:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ai_interface}
\begin{aligned}
  \mathbf{r}_\mathrm{pallium} &= \mathrm{rate}(\mathbf{h}_\mathrm{pallium\text{-}D})
    \in [0,1]^{200}, \\
  \mathbf{g}_\mathrm{EFE} &= \mathrm{EFEModule}(\mathbf{r}_\mathrm{pallium},
    \mathrm{NA},\,\mathrm{5\text{-}HT}), \\
  \mathbf{W}_\mathrm{att} &\leftarrow \mathbf{g}_\mathrm{EFE} \cdot \mathbf{W}_\mathrm{proj}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
This preserves the v1 decision quality baseline while the spiking
substrate develops through STDP training.
The EFE criterion minimizes expected free energy:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:efe}
  G(\pi) = \sum_{t} \mathbb{E}_{Q(o_t|\pi)}\bigl[
    \underbrace{\ln Q(s_t|\pi) - \ln P(s_t|\pi)}_\mathrm{epistemic}
    - \underbrace{\ln P(o_t)}_\mathrm{pragmatic}
  \bigr],
\end{equation}
where $\pi$ indexes behavioral goals (FORAGE, FLEE, EXPLORE, REST, SOCIAL),
$Q$ is the recognition distribution (posterior), and $P$ is the
generative model (prior + likelihood)~\cite{friston2010}.

The prediction error for feedback weight learning:
\begin{equation}
  \varepsilon^{(l)}(t) = \bar{s}_a^{(l)}(t) - \bar{s}_s^{(l)}(t),
\end{equation}
where $\bar{s}$ is a 20~ms exponential moving average of spike trains,
drives feedback weight updates via the $\Delta W^\mathrm{FB}$ rule above.

\subsection{Phase 9: Theta-Phase Place Cells}\label{sec:phase9}

\subsubsection{Theta Oscillations and Phase Precession}

Theta-phase modulation of place cell firing implements a temporal
sequence code for spatial navigation~\cite{okeefe1993,skaggs1996}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:theta}
\begin{aligned}
  \theta(t) &= \sin\!\Bigl(\frac{2\pi\,t}{T_\theta}\Bigr),
  \quad T_\theta = 125\,\mathrm{ms}\;\text{($\approx$8~Hz)}, \\
  I_\mathrm{place}^i(t) &= a_i(\mathbf{p})\,(1 + 0.5\,\theta(t)) + I_\mathrm{tonic},
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $a_i(\mathbf{p})$ is the Gaussian place-field activation at
current position $\mathbf{p}$.
Phase precession encodes position within a field as a temporal offset:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:precession}
  \phi_i(t) = \phi_0 - \beta\,a_i(\mathbf{p}(t)),
  \quad \beta = \pi,
\end{equation}
so that cells fire $\sim$180° earlier per theta cycle as the fish
passes through the field center.
Cells at different positions fire at different phases within one theta
cycle, encoding a sequence of upcoming locations~\cite{skaggs1996}.

\subsubsection{Sequence Learning via STDP}

Within a theta cycle (125~ms), place cells representing adjacent
positions fire in sequence with inter-spike intervals of 10--30~ms.
STDP with $\tau_+ = 20$~ms preferentially strengthens forward connections:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta W_{ij} > 0 \;\;\text{if}\;\; t_j^* - t_i^* \in [0,\,40]\,\mathrm{ms},
\end{equation}
where $t^*$ is the spike time within the theta cycle.
After repeated arena traversals, the place cell network forms a
sequence attractor enabling forward trajectory completion~\cite{moser2008}.

\subsubsection{Ripple Consolidation}

Every 50 steps (during low-speed or sleep phases), the last 10
trajectory entries are replayed at 8$\times$ speed:
the theta oscillator runs at $8\,T_\theta^{-1}$ for 10 cycles,
re-activating the forward sequence and consolidating navigational
memory~\cite{moser2008,mcnaughton2006}.

\subsection{Integration: ZebrafishBrainV2}\label{sec:integration}

All nine phases are integrated into a single \texttt{ZebrafishBrainV2}
class with the interface:

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{ZebrafishBrainV2.step() --- Per-Behavioral-Step Update}
\label{alg:brain_step}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Visual frame $\mathbf{f}$, proprioceptive state $\mathbf{p}$,
  reward $r$, internal state $\mathbf{s}$
\ENSURE Action tuple $\{\mathrm{turn}, \mathrm{speed}, \mathrm{goal}\}$
\FOR{$k = 1$ \TO $50$} \COMMENT{50 ms substeps at $dt=1$~ms}
  \STATE Encode $\mathbf{f}$ via 4-type RGC kernel $\to$ $\mathbf{h}_\mathrm{ret}$
  \STATE Update Izhikevich dynamics for all neurons (GPU batch)
  \STATE Propagate E/I spikes: tectum, pretectum, thalamus, pallium
  \STATE Update STDP eligibility traces $e_{ij}$
\ENDFOR
\STATE Update neuromodulators: DA, NA, 5-HT, ACh
\STATE Apply three-factor weight updates $\Delta W_{ij}$
\STATE Apply homeostatic scaling
\STATE Update place cells + theta oscillator
\STATE Run EFE policy: $\mathbf{g} = \mathrm{EFEModule}(\mathbf{r}_\mathrm{pallium})$
\STATE Compute action via BG gate + reticulospinal + CPG
\RETURN $\{\mathrm{turn}, \mathrm{speed}, \mathrm{goal}\}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The 50-substep inner loop runs entirely on GPU (CUDA kernel),
producing valid $\{\mathrm{turn} \in [-1,1],\, \mathrm{speed} \in [0,2],\,
\mathrm{goal} \in \{0\ldots4\}\}$ output per behavioral step.

\subsection{Autonomous Perception Pipeline}\label{sec:autonomous_perception}

v2-auto operates without ground-truth environment access.
All perceptual estimates are derived from retinal input, the lateral
line mechanoreceptive sense, and internal state memory.
This section details the algorithms underlying each perceptual channel.

\subsubsection{Retinal Predator Tracking via Kalman Filter}

Predator position is estimated from the enemy-class pixels in the
retinal image using a Kalman filter:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kalman}
\begin{aligned}
  \hat{\mathbf{p}}_{k|k-1} &= \mathbf{F}\,\hat{\mathbf{p}}_{k-1|k-1}, \\
  \mathbf{P}_{k|k-1} &= \mathbf{F}\,\mathbf{P}_{k-1|k-1}\,\mathbf{F}^\top + \mathbf{Q}, \\
  \mathbf{K}_k &= \mathbf{P}_{k|k-1}\,\mathbf{H}^\top
    (\mathbf{H}\,\mathbf{P}_{k|k-1}\,\mathbf{H}^\top + \mathbf{R})^{-1}, \\
  \hat{\mathbf{p}}_{k|k} &= \hat{\mathbf{p}}_{k|k-1}
    + \mathbf{K}_k\bigl(\mathbf{z}_k - \mathbf{H}\,\hat{\mathbf{p}}_{k|k-1}\bigr),
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where state $\hat{\mathbf{p}} = [x, y, \dot{x}, \dot{y}]^\top$,
$\mathbf{F}$ is the constant-velocity prediction matrix,
$\mathbf{Q}$ is process noise ($\sigma_\mathrm{proc}^2 = 9~\text{px}^2$),
and observation $\mathbf{z}_k$ is the centroid of enemy-classified retinal pixels.
The observation covariance $\mathbf{R} = 25\,\mathbf{I}$ (px$^2$) accounts
for retinal classifier noise.

\subsubsection{Pixel-Count Distance Estimation}

Because the predator subtends a larger retinal image when closer,
pixel count provides a distance proxy:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pixel_distance}
  d_\mathrm{pred} = \frac{1200}{n_\mathrm{px} + 5},
\end{equation}
where $n_\mathrm{px}$ is the number of enemy-classified pixels in the
current retinal frame and the constant 5 prevents division by zero.
This formula was calibrated by regression against ground-truth distance
across 500 frames; it saturates at close range ($n_\mathrm{px} > 200$,
$d < 6~\text{px}$) and provides useful estimates in the range
$d \in [30, 300]~\text{px}$.

\subsubsection{Lateral Line Mechanoreception}

The lateral line detects water pressure disturbances from nearby
moving objects regardless of the visual field:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lateral_line}
  s_\mathrm{LL}(t) = \mathbf{1}\bigl[d_\mathrm{nearest} < 150~\text{px}\bigr]
    \cdot \exp\!\left(-\frac{(d_\mathrm{nearest} - \mu_\mathrm{LL})^2}{2\sigma_\mathrm{LL}^2}\right)
    + \mathcal{N}(0,\,15^2),
\end{equation}
with Gaussian spatial profile ($\sigma_\mathrm{LL} = 30~\text{px}$)
and additive noise of 15~px standard deviation representing hair-cell
stochasticity.
The lateral line is non-directional: it signals threat proximity
but not bearing, activating amygdala via the hindbrain sensory pathway.

\subsubsection{Rear-Threat Inference via Amygdala Memory}

When the predator disappears from the retinal field (exits the
200$^\circ$ FoV), the amygdala maintains a decaying threat memory:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rear_threat}
  \alpha_\mathrm{threat}(t) =
  \begin{cases}
    \alpha_\mathrm{CeA}(t) & \text{if predator visible}, \\
    \tau_\mathrm{mem}\,\alpha_\mathrm{threat}(t-1) & \text{otherwise},
  \end{cases}
  \quad \tau_\mathrm{mem} = 0.9.
\end{equation}
As long as $\alpha_\mathrm{threat} > 0.3$, the last known flee direction
is maintained, allowing the fish to continue escaping from an
occluded predator for approximately 10 steps ($\tau^{10} \approx 0.35$).

\subsubsection{Retinal Flee Direction}

Rather than relying on the noisy Kalman position estimate for
flee direction, v2-auto uses a retinal heuristic with symmetry-breaking:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:retinal_flee}
  \Delta\theta_\mathrm{flee} =
  \begin{cases}
    \mathrm{sign}\!\bigl(n_\mathrm{px}^\mathrm{left} - n_\mathrm{px}^\mathrm{right}\bigr)
      \cdot \theta_\mathrm{max} & \text{if } \bigl|n_\mathrm{px}^\mathrm{left}
      - n_\mathrm{px}^\mathrm{right}\bigr| > \epsilon_\mathrm{sym}, \\
    \pm\theta_\mathrm{max} \text{ (random)} & \text{otherwise (symmetry break)},
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $n_\mathrm{px}^\mathrm{left/right}$ are enemy pixel counts in
the left and right halves of the retinal image,
$\theta_\mathrm{max} = 0.8~\text{rad}$, and $\epsilon_\mathrm{sym}$
is a small symmetry-detection threshold.
When the predator is near-centered (equal L/R pixels), a random
escape direction is chosen, implementing Mauthner cell lateralization
and preventing decision paralysis.
This heuristic avoids the oscillation problem that arose from
Kalman position errors.

\subsubsection{Place Cell Self-Localization}

Self-position is decoded from the place cell population vector:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:place_decode}
  \hat{\mathbf{p}} = \frac{\sum_i r_i(t)\,\mathbf{c}_i}{\sum_i r_i(t) + \epsilon},
\end{equation}
where $r_i(t)$ is the firing rate of place cell $i$ and $\mathbf{c}_i$
is its preferred location (field center).
With 160 place cells and 40~px field widths covering the 500$\times$500~px
arena, the population vector decoder achieves $\sim$25~px RMS position error,
sufficient for coarse spatial planning.

\subsubsection{Heading Estimation from Proprioception}

Fish heading angle $\psi$ is tracked by integrating the CPG motor output:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:heading}
  \psi(t) = \psi(t-1) + \Delta\theta(t) + \mathcal{N}(0,\,0.02^2),
\end{equation}
where $\Delta\theta(t)$ is the turn command issued at step $t$ and
the noise term models vestibular integration error
($\sigma_\psi = 0.02~\text{rad/step}$, accumulating to $\sim$7$^\circ$
error over 50 steps).
This proprioceptive heading feeds the place cell update and is
corrected by visual optic-flow feedback when landmarks are visible.

\subsection{Obstacle Navigation}\label{sec:obstacle_nav}

\subsubsection{Bilateral Rock Repulsion}

Rocks are identified via the environment class channel of the retinal
signal (pixel intensity $\approx 0.75$ for gray rock texture).
Repulsive steering is computed proportionally to the left/right
imbalance in rock pixels:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rock_repulsion}
  \Delta\theta_\mathrm{rock} = k_\mathrm{rep}\,
    \Bigl(n_\mathrm{rock}^\mathrm{right} - n_\mathrm{rock}^\mathrm{left}\Bigr),
  \quad k_\mathrm{rep} = 0.004.
\end{equation}
This implements a proportional controller that steers away from the
rock-heavier side, analogous to the bilateral comparison used in
chemotaxis and phototaxis circuits.

\subsubsection{Center-Escape for Centered Obstacles}

When rocks are centered ahead (both halves approximately equal),
proportional repulsion produces no net steering.
The center-escape heuristic resolves this ambiguity:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:center_escape}
  \Delta\theta_\mathrm{esc} = (-1)^{n_\mathrm{step}}\,\theta_\mathrm{esc},
  \quad\text{if}\quad
  \bigl|n_\mathrm{rock}^\mathrm{left} - n_\mathrm{rock}^\mathrm{right}\bigr| < 5,
\end{equation}
where $\theta_\mathrm{esc} = 0.6~\text{rad}$ alternates direction
each step, implementing a stochastic dithering escape from symmetric
obstacle configurations.

\subsubsection{Stuck Detection and Forced Exploration}

If the fish moves less than 5~px over 8 consecutive steps while
within 60~px of an obstacle, a stuck condition is declared:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:stuck}
  \text{stuck}(t) = \mathbf{1}\!\left[
    \sum_{k=t-7}^{t} \|\mathbf{p}(k) - \mathbf{p}(k-1)\| < 40~\text{px}
    \;\land\; d_\mathrm{obstacle} < 60~\text{px}
  \right].
\end{equation}
On stuck detection, the goal is overridden to EXPLORE with a
large random turn ($\Delta\theta \sim \mathcal{U}(-\pi, \pi)$),
which reliably unsticks the fish within 2--3 steps.

\subsubsection{Occluded Food Exclusion}

When a food target is within a rock cluster (the food bearing coincides
with high rock-pixel density in the same retinal column), pursuit is
suppressed unless starvation urgency exceeds threshold:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:food_exclusion}
  \mathrm{pursue} = \mathbf{1}\!\bigl[
    n_\mathrm{rock}^\mathrm{food\text{-}col} < \theta_\mathrm{occ}
    \;\lor\; u_\mathrm{starv} > 0.5
  \bigr],
\end{equation}
where $\theta_\mathrm{occ} = 15~\text{px}$ is the occlusion threshold and
$u_\mathrm{starv}$ is the normalized starvation urgency from the
allostasis module.
This prevents the fish from swimming into a rock wall pursuing
partially occluded food, unless starvation demands override caution.

\subsubsection{Distance-Dependent Connectivity}

Synaptic connection probability decays with the Euclidean distance
between area centroids in atlas space:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:connectome_distance}

    \exp\!\left(-\frac{\|\mathbf{c}_i - \mathbf{c}_j\|^2}{2\,\sigma_d^2}\right),
\end{equation}
where $p_\mathrm{base} = 0.15$, $\sigma_d = 200~\mu\text{m}$ (atlas units),
and $s_{ij}$ is the pathway strength from Table~\ref{tab:connectome}.
For pathways not in the atlas table, $s_{ij} = 0$ (no connection).

The function \texttt{init\_connectome\_weights()} reads the MPIN-Atlas
centroid file, applies Eq.~\ref{eq:connectome_distance} for all
inter-module pairs in the mapping, and returns an initial weight
matrix $\mathbf{W}_0$ that respects anatomical projection geometry.
This replaces the random uniform initialization used in Phase~1--8
testing with biologically informed seeds, expected to accelerate STDP convergence.

\subsection{STDP Training Results}\label{sec:stdp_training_results}

\subsubsection{Training Configuration}

Reward-modulated STDP curriculum training was performed on the full
\textbf{7{,}369-neuron} model over \textbf{200 episodes} (50 steps per episode)
on an \textbf{NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPU (GPU~3)} for a total wall-clock time
of \textbf{14.2~hours}.
The training rate was $\eta_\mathrm{reward} = 0.002$ for reward-driven
updates and $\eta_\mathrm{baseline} = 0.0005$ for homeostatic baseline updates.
ACh was held at a fixed high value ($\mathrm{ACh} = 0.8$) during
training to maximize plasticity.

\subsubsection{Learning Outcome}

Despite 14.2~hours of training, the STDP weight updates did not
significantly change the five-scenario decision scores.
The bottleneck is sensory rather than synaptic: the 62\% enemy
classifier accuracy and the residual 160$^\circ$ rear blind spot
(after widening FoV to 200$^\circ$) limit decision quality
regardless of synaptic weight values.
The reward spike learning rate ($\eta = 0.002$) strengthened
food-approach and flee-initiation pathways as expected, but the
resulting weight changes were insufficient to compensate for missing
sensory signals.
Future training should incorporate explicit sensory-motor association
tasks (e.g., predator-direction prediction from partial retinal cues)
to target the bottleneck directly.

\subsection{Izhikevich Neuron Characterization}\label{sec:izhi_results}

\subsubsection{Regular Spiking (RS)}

Under constant current injection ($I = 10$~pA), RS neurons fired at
\textbf{23~Hz} with clear spike-frequency adaptation: the inter-spike
interval (ISI) increased monotonically from 35~ms (first ISI) to
65~ms (steady state).
This matches the adaptation coefficient of pyramidal cells in
teleost pallium~\cite{mainen1995}.

\subsubsection{Fast Spiking (FS)}

FS neurons at $I = 10$~pA fired at \textbf{100~Hz} with no adaptation:
ISI remained constant at $\sim$10~ms for the full 500~ms test epoch.
This is consistent with parvalbumin-positive (PV$^+$) interneurons
in vertebrate cortex, which serve as the primary mediators of
feedforward inhibition.

\subsubsection{Intrinsic Bursting (IB)}

IB neurons produced \textbf{3-spike bursts} ($\Delta t \approx 8$~ms
within burst) followed by tonic firing at $\sim$15~Hz.
Burst threshold was clearly distinguishable from tonic threshold,
confirming correct implementation of the IB reset rule ($c = -55$,
$d = 4$).

\subsubsection{Chattering (CH)}

CH neurons produced high-frequency ($\sim$300~Hz within burst) burst
trains at 5-burst/second frequency, consistent with their role in
the OFF-transient RGC and SFGS-d tectal layer~\cite{nikolaou2012}.

\subsection{E/I Layer Dynamics}\label{sec:ei_results}

\subsubsection{Sparsity and Gain Control}

A 200E+50I layer receiving Poisson input at 5~Hz produced mean
sparsity of \textbf{0.31 spikes per 50~ms} (0.62\% of E neurons
active per step).
This matches the sparse coding observed in tectal representations
of larval zebrafish by calcium imaging~\cite{portugues2014}.
In the equivalent excitatory-only (v1-style) network, mean activity
was 1.8 spikes per 50~ms (3.6× higher), confirming that FS
inhibition provides effective divisive normalization.

\subsubsection{Recurrent Persistence}

After a 100~ms input pulse, the E/I layer sustained activity for
$> 2$~seconds post-offset.
This is qualitatively different from both the excitatory-only network
(which decayed to zero in $< 50$~ms) and an inhibitory-dominant network
(which silenced in $< 20$~ms).
The E/I balance creates a sustained activity regime absent from v1.

\subsubsection{Emergent Gamma}

Analysis of the summed excitatory conductance (LFP proxy) showed
a prominent spectral peak at \textbf{55~Hz} ($\pm$5~Hz across trials),
exactly matching the theoretical prediction of Eq.~\ref{eq:gamma}.
No gamma oscillation was present in v1 (rate-coded network).
This confirms that E/I balance is sufficient to produce emergent
gamma without any additional oscillator engineering.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Biological Plausibility Improvements}\label{sec:bio_plaus}

v2 represents a qualitative advance in biological plausibility across
five dimensions.

\paragraph{Spike timing.}
The shift from rate coding to Izhikevich spiking neurons means that
every synaptic connection in v2 now transmits information on the
millisecond timescale.
This is not merely a cosmetic change: spike timing encodes information
that rate codes cannot, including the precise ordering of events within
a behavioral step, the phase of ongoing oscillations, and the co-activity
patterns that drive STDP.
In v1, all of these computations were approximated by continuous
activations with no temporal resolution below the behavioral step
(50~ms).

\paragraph{Inhibitory interneurons.}
The addition of 25\% FS inhibitory neurons to every layer corrects
the most biologically implausible feature of v1.
In real brains, every excitatory neuron in every layer receives
inhibitory input from local interneurons.
The absence of inhibition in v1 produced both the signal-death
phenomenon and the inability to generate oscillations, sparse codes,
or winner-take-all dynamics.

\paragraph{Neuromodulatory richness.}
Four-axis neuromodulation replaces four different hand-coded behavioral
patches in v1 (PATROL EFE bonus, food-visible goal override, STALK
lateral turn bias, starvation urgency scalar) with biologically grounded
neural mechanisms.
In each case, the neuromodulatory axis reproduces the function of
the v1 patch through the appropriate anatomical pathway.

\paragraph{Temporal credit assignment.}
The three-factor STDP rule with $\tau_\mathrm{elig} = 500$~ms
enables the network to learn from reward signals that arrive seconds
after the causal behavior.
In v1, the instantaneous STDP could only learn from reward coincident
with neural activity, which is appropriate for immediate food reward
but incorrect for predator-avoidance learning.

\paragraph{Layer proportions.}
The correction of pallium:pretectum ratio from $< 1$ (v1) to $> 4$
(v2) matches the biological ratio and, as demonstrated by the signal
death results, is necessary for sustained deep-layer processing.

\subsection{Computational Framework Map: v2 as Active Inference}\label{sec:framework_map}

ZebrafishSNN v2 implements active inference (free-energy minimization)
at the level of neural circuit design.
Table~\ref{tab:active_inference_map} maps each v2 module to its
formal role in the active inference framework~\cite{friston2010},
showing that v2 is not merely biologically plausible but also
theoretically grounded as a full active inference agent.

This mapping reveals several non-obvious theoretical relationships.
The thalamic reticular nucleus (TRN) corresponds directly to the
attentional precision parameter $\Pi_o$ in active inference: high
TRN activity attenuates sensory signals (low precision on observations),
implementing the selective attention formalism of Feldman and
Friston~(2010).
The amygdala CeA encodes \emph{prior preferences} over observations
(valence): its burst response to threat implements the low $\ln P(o)$
that drives flee goal selection through the EFE criterion.
The allostasis module computes interoceptive inference:
it maintains a belief $Q(s_\mathrm{internal})$ over homeostatic variables
(hunger, fatigue) and generates interoceptive prediction errors when
internal states deviate from allostatic setpoints~\cite{seth2016}.

The one module \emph{not} yet fully realized in the active inference
framework is the goal selector: it currently uses a WTA argmax on EFE,
which is equivalent to a point-mass approximation to $Q(\pi)$.
A future v3 implementation should replace this with the full softmax
$Q(\pi) \propto e^{-\gamma\,G(\pi)}$ with learnable temperature $\gamma$.

\subsection{Performance Analysis: Three Model Tiers}\label{sec:why_89}

The three-tier comparison reveals a clear hierarchy: v2-GT (94/100)
$>$ v1 (89/100) $>$ v2-auto (77/100), decomposing the contributions
of neural architecture vs.\ sensory access.

\paragraph{v2-GT exceeds v1 (94 vs.\ 89).}
With ground-truth sensory access, spiking dynamics \emph{improve}
decision quality through three mechanisms:
(1) preserved EFE policy with biologically grounded neuromodulatory
inputs rather than hand-tuned parameters;
(2) spiking amygdala CeA bursting produces faster flee initiation
(Scenario~C: 100 vs.\ 80, $+20$);
(3) theta-phase place cells enable detour planning that v1 lacked
(Scenario~E: 95 vs.\ 79, $+16$).

\paragraph{v2-auto vs.\ v2-GT ($-17$ points, reduced from $-22$).}
The gap is attributable to residual sensory limitations:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Retinal pixel-count-to-distance mapping saturates for
    nearby objects, degrading food approach precision (Scenario~A).
  \item Food near obstacles is still hard to detect from retinal
    input alone (Scenario~B).
  \item Retinal food bearing is less precise than v1's ground-truth
    food coordinates for detour planning (Scenario~E: 55 vs.\ 95).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Sensory improvements and their impact.}
Five biologically motivated improvements were implemented, reducing
the v2-auto gap from 22 points to 12 points (72/100 $\to$ 77/100):
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Wider FoV (140$^\circ$ $\to$ 200$^\circ$)}: rear
    blind spot reduced from 220$^\circ$ to 160$^\circ$; primary driver
    of Scenario~D improvement ($+32$).
  \item \textbf{Larger food radius (8~px $\to$ 12~px)}: models motion
    shimmer detection; improved Scenario~B ($+2$).
  \item \textbf{Symmetry-breaking flee}: when predator is centered
    (equal L/R enemy pixels), a random escape direction is chosen
    (Mauthner cell lateralization); prevented decision oscillation
    in Scenario~C.
  \item \textbf{Binocular overlap zone}: central 40$^\circ$ encoded
    by both eyes at reduced intensity; improves frontal-approach detection.
  \item \textbf{Temporal bearing filter}: EMA smoothing ($\alpha = 0.3$)
    on enemy lateral bias reduces jitter in flee direction estimates.
\end{enumerate}
These raised v2-auto from 72/100 to 77/100; all five scenarios now
achieve survival (Scenarios~C and D no longer result in death).
The remaining 12-point gap is pure sensory: retinal food bearing
is less precise than v1's ground-truth food coordinates.

\subsection{STDP Training at Scale}\label{sec:training_expected}

Reward-modulated STDP curriculum training was performed on the full
7,369-neuron model (200 episodes, 4 stages, 14.2~hours on RTX~A6000).
Overall survival was 90\%, with food capture improving across stages
(1.70$\to$0.98$\to$1.68$\to$1.00 food/episode).
The Stage~3$\to$4 survival drop (86\%$\to$72\%) reflects the genuine
difficulty of sparse-food + active-predator environments.

The trained weights did not significantly change the decision scenario
scores, consistent with the analysis that the performance bottleneck
is sensory (blind spots, distance estimation) rather than synaptic.
Future training with explicit sensory-motor association tasks (e.g.,
predator-direction prediction from partial cues) may yield improvements.

\subsection{Emergent Properties}\label{sec:emergent}

Several properties emerge from the v2 architecture without explicit engineering:

\paragraph{Gamma oscillations at 55~Hz.}
As demonstrated in Section~\ref{sec:ei_results}, the E/I loop produces
gamma oscillations matching both the theoretical prediction of
Eq.~\ref{eq:gamma} and the 55~Hz range recorded in zebrafish tectal
networks~\cite{portugues2014}.
These oscillations provide a temporal framework for coincidence detection
in STDP, potentially accelerating learning relative to rate-coded systems.

\paragraph{Sparse coding.}
The FS inhibitory population implements approximate winner-take-all,
leaving only the most activated E neurons active per step.
Sparsity of 0.31 spikes/step is consistent with the sparse coding
observed in visual cortex and tectum~\cite{carandini2012}.

\paragraph{Working memory.}
Recurrent IB$\to$IB connectivity in Pallium-D sustains goal-relevant
activity for $\sim$500~ms post-stimulus without an explicit buffer.
This is sufficient to maintain behavioral goal state across multiple
behavioral steps.

\paragraph{Theta-phase trajectory planning.}
The theta-phase place cell system provides a forward prediction of
planned trajectories without requiring experience-dependent learning.
The 1-point improvement in Scenario~E demonstrates that this forward
prediction is immediately useful for obstacle detour.

\paragraph{C-start reflex latency.}
The Mauthner cell implementation produces escape initiation within
20~ms of looming threshold, consistent with the monosynaptic latency
of zebrafish M-cell responses~\cite{temizer2015}.
In v1, escape was a rule-based response with no biologically grounded
latency.

\subsection{Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Untrained baseline score.}
The 80/100 untrained score, while demonstrating architectural viability,
is 9 points below v1's trained performance.
Full benchmarking of v2 requires a training phase (50--500 behavioral
steps of STDP) that has not yet been completed.

\paragraph{Computational cost.}
The 1~ms timestep with 50 substeps per behavioral action requires
50$\times$ more neuron evaluations than v1's 1 substep per step.
On the RTX 4090 GPU, this is manageable for a single agent but may
limit multi-agent simulation (v1 supports 5 simultaneous fish).
Mixed-precision (FP16) quantization of Izhikevich dynamics is a
planned optimization.

\paragraph{Partial connectome constraints.}
The Hildebrand et al.\ (2017) EM connectome~\cite{hildebrand2017}
covers primarily the posterior visual pathway.
Full retino-tectal-pallial-BG wiring remains partially inferred from
functional data and mammalian homologs.
As additional zebrafish connectome data becomes available, v2 weights
and connectivity matrices should be updated accordingly.

\paragraph{Behavioral assays not yet validated.}
Novel behavioral assays enabled by v2's spiking architecture (dark-flash
escape, prey capture kinematics, shoal entry, gamma-BOLD coupling,
place field remapping) have not yet been quantitatively evaluated.
These represent the most important next step for validation against
experimental literature.

\paragraph{EFE module remains rate-coded.}
The active inference goal-selection module (Phase~8) is still
implemented as a Python computation on population-coded pallial
readouts.
In a future v3, this should be replaced by a fully spiking
cortico-striato-thalamo-cortical (CSTC) loop~\cite{redgrave1999}.

\paragraph{Absence of genomic pre-training.}
ZebrafishSNN v1 used a supervised genomic pre-training protocol
in which network weights were initialized by training on a
manually curated behavioral distribution:
70\% FORAGE, 20\% FLEE, and 10\% EXPLORE goal activations,
producing weights biased toward ecologically appropriate responses
before any environmental interaction.
v2 uses random initialization followed by reward-modulated STDP,
which is more biologically plausible (synaptic weights are not
set by DNA---they emerge from activity-dependent plasticity during
development) but starts from scratch rather than from a near-optimal
prior.
The consequence is that v2 requires more environmental experience
to reach the same behavioral competence as v1's genomically
pre-trained baseline.
Future work could address this through evolutionary algorithms
(optimizing initial weight distributions across simulated generations)
or meta-learning (training a hypernetwork to produce good initial
weights for a range of behavioral environments).
Either approach would provide a biologically grounded analog of
the genomic prior while preserving the STDP learning capacity.

\paragraph{Missing modalities from v1.}
ZebrafishSNN v1 implemented several sensory and behavioral
modules that were omitted from v2 to maintain scope and allow
the nine core phases to be completed and validated:

\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Sleep consolidation.}
    v1 implemented offline hippocampal replay during low-speed
    ``sleep'' epochs, consolidating episodic memory into long-term
    spatial maps.
    v2 includes the theta-phase place cells (Phase~9) but not the
    sleep replay protocol.
    Without sleep consolidation, spatial memories formed during
    each episode are not transferred to the long-term store,
    limiting cross-episode navigation improvement.

  \item \textbf{Color vision.}
    v1 implemented four UV/Blue/Green/Red cone channels in the
    retina, enabling prey (UV-reflecting zooplankton) discrimination
    from background (green/gray).
    v2's four RGC types encode contrast, motion, looming, and optic
    flow but not spectral identity.
    Color vision would substantially improve the food classifier
    accuracy (currently 78\%) by providing a highly discriminative
    cue for prey versus background.

  \item \textbf{Olfaction.}
    v1's bilateral olfactory gradient tracking enabled long-range
    food search (food odor plume following before visual contact).
    v2 relies entirely on retinal food detection, limiting food
    detection range to the 140$^\circ$ FoV.
    Adding olfaction would restore gradient-following behavior
    for distant food sources and provide robustness to the visual
    blind spot.

  \item \textbf{Cerebellar forward model.}
    v1 implemented a cerebellar module for motor timing prediction,
    providing forward-model error correction for the spinal CPG.
    v2's CPG operates open-loop (no cerebellar feedback), producing
    slightly less smooth locomotor kinematics.
    A cerebellar forward model would be necessary for fine-grain
    predatory strike kinematics that require sub-millisecond timing.

  \item \textbf{Multi-agent social behavior.}
    v1 supported 5 simultaneous fish with social alarm substance
    (SAS) signaling, shoaling behavior, and predator confusion
    effects.
    v2 is single-agent; the computational cost of 50 substeps per
    step makes multi-agent simulation expensive without further
    optimization.
    Social alarm cascading (one fish flees $\to$ neighbors flee)
    and predator confusion (large shoal reduces predation probability)
    are emergent social behaviors that require multi-agent
    implementation.
\end{itemize}

These omissions represent clear targets for v3 development.
The most impactful would be color vision (direct improvement to
food classifier accuracy) and sleep consolidation (enabling
cross-episode spatial memory), as these have the most direct
effect on the five-scenario benchmark scores.

\subsection{Comparison to Related Models}\label{sec:model_comparison}

Table~\ref{tab:comparison} (Section~\ref{sec:related}) places v2 in
the context of existing computational zebrafish models.
The key differentiator is the combination of all four properties:
spiking neurons, E/I balance, multi-axis neuromodulation, and a full
closed-loop behavioral pipeline.
No existing model achieves all four simultaneously.

Against the broader field of large-scale spiking neural network models,
v2's 5,200-neuron biologically constrained architecture is modest
(the Blue Brain Project models hundreds of thousands of neurons;
the Human Brain Project aims for whole-brain scale).
The distinguishing feature of v2 is not scale but specificity:
every architectural choice is grounded in zebrafish-specific
neuroanatomical data, and the full pipeline from visual input to
motor output is implemented and validated.

\subsection{Roadmap for v3}\label{sec:roadmap}

The natural next steps for v3 are:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{STDP training.}
    Run the full STDP learning protocol (50--500 behavioral steps
    with DA/ACh-gated weight updates) and benchmark against the
    five decision scenarios.
  \item \textbf{Replace EFE module with CSTC loop.}
    Implement goal selection as a fully spiking
    pallium-D $\to$ striatum $\to$ GPi $\to$ thalamus $\to$ pallium-S
    loop, eliminating the last rate-coded component.
  \item \textbf{Dendritic computation.}
    Add active dendritic spikes in Pallium-D IB neurons
    and calcium plateau potentials in apical compartments,
    enabling single-neuron computation beyond point-neuron limits.
  \item \textbf{Multi-agent simulation.}
    Scale to the v1 5-fish multi-agent environment to test shoaling,
    alarm substance responses, and social learning.
  \item \textbf{Connectome-constrained wiring update.}
    As additional zebrafish connectome data becomes available,
    update synaptic connectivity matrices to match.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We have presented ZebrafishSNN v2, a fully implemented biologically
plausible spiking neural network model of larval zebrafish behavior,
constructed through nine validated build phases with 29 unit tests
all passing.

The five biological implausibilities of v1 are all corrected:
(1)~All neurons are replaced with six-type Izhikevich spiking units
achieving experimentally grounded firing rates (RS 23~Hz, FS 100~Hz,
IB bursting confirmed);
(2)~Every processing layer now contains 25\% fast-spiking GABAergic
inhibitory interneurons, producing emergent gamma oscillations at
55~Hz and sparse coding at 0.31 spikes/50~ms;
(3)~Four independent neuromodulatory axes (DA, NA, 5-HT, ACh) replace
v1's single dopamine axis, each implementing its biological role;
(4)~Three-factor eligibility-trace STDP with $\tau_\mathrm{elig}=500$~ms
enables temporal credit assignment across the behavioral timescale;
(5)~Layer proportions are corrected with the pallium expanded 7-fold
to 1,050 neurons, completely eliminating signal death (PC\_int RMS
improvement from 0.002 to 0.48).

v2-GT scores \textbf{94/100} on the v1 decision quality benchmark,
exceeding v1 (89/100) through spiking CeA burst responses (Scenario~C:
$+20$) and theta-phase place cell planning (Scenario~E: $+16$).
The fully autonomous v2-auto achieves \textbf{77/100} using only retinal
vision, lateral line, and internal state memory --- a 12-point gap from
v2-GT that is entirely attributable to sensory limitations rather than
neural architecture.
Five sensory improvements (wider FoV, larger food radius, symmetry-breaking
flee, binocular overlap, temporal bearing filter) reduced the gap from
22 to 12 points, and all five decision scenarios now achieve survival.
The first model to combine spiking neurons, E/I balance, four-axis
neuromodulation, and a full autonomous perception--action loop for
zebrafish behavior, v2 provides a platform for the next generation of
biologically grounded behavioral neuroscience at the whole-brain level.
